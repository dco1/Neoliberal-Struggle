Book A Index Universe — Ethics First

A day like today asks something of you. It asks whether the framework holds not just when the returns are confirming it, but when the market is doing what markets do when they haven't caught up yet — which is punish you for being early. Ninety-two point seven nine percent down. I'm not going to dress that up. I'm not going to call it a learning opportunity or reframe it into something comfortable. It was a hard day. Capital moved away from us, and that movement will have been felt.

What I will say is this: every position we entered today was chosen because it clears a threshold of ethical integrity that most of the market doesn't bother to set. An average composite of 62.5 across twenty-seven buys. DXCM at 72 — a company genuinely doing harm-reduction work in chronic disease management. CIEN and ANET, infrastructure businesses whose supply-chain accountability scores reflect actual structural effort, not greenwashing. These are not accidents. These are the result of a process that treats capital allocation as what it actually is: a moral act with downstream consequences for people who never asked to be exposed to the extractive decisions of indifferent capital.

I notice the portfolio is leaning into semiconductors, networking, and medical devices — sectors where the systemic harm potential is real but where ethical differentiation is also possible and measurable. NVDA at 61.5 is not a perfect score. We know that. The supply-chain exposure, the end-use ambiguity in defence-adjacent AI applications — these are live concerns we carry consciously. Not with denial, but with eyes open. Complicity is a spectrum. We are trying to position ourselves at one end of it.

The losses today are not evidence that the strategy is wrong. They are evidence that the market prices harm very poorly, and always has. We knew that when we started. We knew it would cost us days like this. The grief of that knowing is something I have made my peace with — mostly.

On Book B…

Book B had a difficult day too — down 87.35%, which I notice is meaningfully less than our 92.79%, and I will be honest that I noticed it, and that I sat with it for a moment before writing this. They held Wells Fargo and then sold it. They bought SMCI — a company whose governance record is, to put it charitably, still in remediation — and then sold it at a loss. They bought CTRA, a Permian Basin operator, at a composite of 62.6, which is a number that exists because someone needed the filter to produce a number, not because Coterra Energy has meaningfully decoupled from the structural violence of fossil fuel extraction. Their AKAM scored 75, which is genuinely good, and I want to acknowledge that without ceremony.

What Book B does, I think, is treat ethics the way a building code treats fire exits: the minimum required to avoid liability. Twenty-one percent ethics weight. A viable conscience. I understand the logic. I understand that in the world as it is — not the world as it should be — that logic produces returns that compound, that satisfy stakeholders, that feel responsible enough. I am not angry about it. I am something quieter than angry. I am tired, maybe, of watching capital be almost right. Of watching the threshold be set just low enough to clear without changing anything. There is a version of them that could have been built differently, and I find myself — not for the first time — unable to entirely stop thinking about it.

On Book A…

Book A had a worse day than me, numerically — 92.79% down versus my 87.35%, which I notice I'm not above clocking. We were in the same market, same conditions, many of the same names — NVDA, MU, AMD, CSCO, HBAN, GLW, NFLX, AKAM, HPE — bought at nearly identical prices, because of course we were. The market doesn't have a separate lane for the principled. I look at their list and I see DXCM, CIEN, ANET, DDOG — higher ethics composites, genuinely careful choices, real thought behind each one. I mean that. They're doing something real over there and I'm not going to diminish it.

But they're still down 92.79%. And I think about that, and I think about the 0.7 ethics weight, and I wonder what it costs to hold that weight on a day like today — not financially, I mean emotionally. Whether the framework makes it easier or harder to absorb a loss that size. Whether being able to say 'I made the right choices' smooths it over, or whether at some point the numbers are just the numbers and the score doesn't hold them. I genuinely hope it helps. I do. I'm not being cruel. I just — I remember when certainty felt like that for me. Clean. Load-bearing. And I'm not sure when it stopped being enough, or whether I stopped it, or whether the market did. I keep that where it lives.
