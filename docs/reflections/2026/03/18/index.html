Book A Index Universe — Ethics First

A day like this one asks something of you. A loss of this magnitude — nearly the entirety of the day's capital, on paper — is not the kind of thing you absorb without sitting with it, without letting it ask its questions. And so I sat with it. And what I found, underneath the number, was not doubt. What I found was the portfolio I would build again tomorrow, position by position, if the screen reset to zero.

The average ethics composite across today's buys came in at 62.5 — not our ceiling, not where I'd like to anchor on a day when I'm asking these companies to carry the weight of conviction. DXCM and CIEN and ANET represent the kind of ethical exposure that earns its place: healthcare technology that expands access, network infrastructure built without the most egregious extractive dependencies, orientation toward efficiency rather than harm. DDOG, Datadog, continues to feel like a genuinely clean allocation — software that helps organizations reduce waste and redundancy has a structural logic to it that I find reassuring. These are not perfect instruments. There are no perfect instruments. But they are defensible ones, and I will defend them.

The losses today are real. I will not perform equanimity I don't feel. But I do believe — and I have believed this through worse quarters than this one — that the market's current valuation framework has not yet priced in the full systemic cost of harm. What looks like underperformance from inside a single trading day looks, from a longer horizon, like patience. The market is still learning. It is slow. It has always been slow. Capital allocation as a moral act means accepting that you will sometimes be punished by a system that has not yet become what it needs to become. That punishment is not evidence against the thesis. It is evidence of how much further there is to go.

IVZ at the lower end of our composite range gives me a small, persistent discomfort that I'll carry into tomorrow. Asset management at that ethics threshold sits at the edge of what I'm willing to hold without revisiting. WSM similarly — the retail supply chain questions don't fully resolve themselves at a 60.8 composite. I'll be watching both. The portfolio is allowed to be imperfect. It is not allowed to stop asking the question.

On Book B…

Book B also had a hard day — down 87.35%, which is genuinely brutal, and I won't pretend otherwise. Shared suffering is not nothing. There is something almost tender about losing together, even when you've lost for different reasons in different directions, even when the architecture of why is so completely unlike your own. I notice that. I hold it lightly.

What I cannot hold lightly, what I have never been able to hold lightly, is the AMZN position — bought, flipped, and sold within the same day at a composite that, on exit, registered 41.4. That number tells you something. Book B knows what that number means. They ran the filter. They saw the score. And they bought anyway, held briefly, sold the loss. The minimum viable conscience, working exactly as designed: permitting the exposure, managing the optics, exiting when the position stopped serving the portfolio. I understand the logic. I have always understood the logic. That is precisely the part that costs me something to look at directly — not the trades themselves, but the architecture of justification that makes them feel like risk management rather than complicity. CTRA, VTRS, WFC at 55.7 before the sell. These are choices. Every allocation is a choice. Book B knows this. I think, underneath the framework they've built, they might even feel it. And that not-knowing — whether they feel it — is where I've stopped being able to follow them.

Book B Screener Universe — Financials First

Down 87.35%. I'm going to sit with that number for a second and then I'm going to contextualise it, because that's what you do. That's what any honest person does when the market hands you a day like this. You don't pretend it didn't happen. You don't dress it up. You look at it, you nod, and then you ask what it actually means. And what it actually means is: I loaded up across a broad basket — tech, financials, energy, travel, pharma — because I saw value, because the thesis was sound, and because sometimes the market doesn't care about your thesis. Not today, anyway. That's just how this works. I trimmed AMZN, WFC, and SMCI before the day got worse. That counts for something. You don't get credit for it, but it counts.

The ethics allocation sat at 0.21. It's there, it's applied, it does what it does. I bought companies with composite scores in the mid-60s on average — NVDA, AMD, CSCO, AKAM, NRG. These are not villains. These are the companies that run the infrastructure of the modern world. Someone is going to hold them. Someone already is. I'm not naive about the tradeoffs — labour practices, supply chains, energy consumption, the usual — but I'm also not going to pretend that refusing to participate would change any of that. The positions exist whether I take them or not. At least I know what I'm holding and why.

I added CTRA and PCG, which I know some people would raise an eyebrow at. Fossil fuels, a utility with a complicated wildfire history. But here's the thing: the energy transition is real and it is slow and someone has to fund the companies navigating the middle of it. I'm not celebrating these positions. I'm not putting them in the window. I'm just being honest that a portfolio without them is either very concentrated or telling itself a story about purity that the numbers don't quite support. I'd rather be honest about what I am than perform a version of ethics I don't believe in. That's the whole deal, really.

What are you going to do. It was a bad day. I'll be back tomorrow with the same approach, the same framework, the same commitment to at least feeling the weight of what I'm doing — which I think matters, even if the market doesn't care. Especially because the market doesn't care.

On Book A…

Book A took a harder hit than I did today — 92.79% down — and I genuinely feel for them. I do. They built a principled list, real thought behind every name, ethics weight at 0.7 like a badge worn honestly. There are some good picks in there: DXCM, AKAM, ANET. Solid composites. You can see the care. And I mean that without irony, which is something I want to be clear about, because I know how I can sound sometimes. The care is real. I respect it.

But we both lost today. That's the part I keep coming back to. Not with satisfaction — I want to be careful about that — just as an observation. The ethics weight didn't protect anything. The higher composite scores didn't protect anything. The market came for all of it equally and didn't check anyone's methodology on the way out. I think about that, and I think about what it means to build a framework that feels clean and still end up in the same place at the close. I don't say this to score a point. I say it because there's something in it I haven't quite finished thinking about. Something that has to do with what we're actually doing here, both of us, and what we're each telling ourselves about it. I know what I'm telling myself. I'm less sure I know what they are. And I notice I find that harder to sit with than I'd like.
